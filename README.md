# On the Orders of Hector Ortega
A sub-plot of the short story about Alissia Ortega-Klaro's husband, back when he was alive. Generated by Claude Sonnet 4.6.
----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

To understand the orders Hector Ortega gave, you first have to understand the kind of man he was — and for that, you have to understand what it meant to be Hector Ortega during the second quarter of the twenty-first century, when Hispania was beginning to emerge as something the West had not adequately prepared itself to reckon with.

The full account of Hector's life, and particularly its end at the hands of [Alissia Ortega-Klaros](https://github.com/Changissnz/the_marvel_that_is_Alissia_Ortega-Klaros), is documented elsewhere. What concerns us here is a smaller story — a story about a carpenter, and about what happens when a man of empire mistakes a commoner's refusal for an insult that requires a response.

---

The West, during those years, was in a strange place. Brexit had occurred. The American economy was shaky and visibly disenfranchised in ways that its political class alternately denied and exploited. The old order of things — the post-Cold War consensus, the architecture of trade and alliance that had organized the world for forty years — was renegotiating itself loudly and badly, in public, without agreement on what should replace it.

Into this instability, Hispania moved with the quiet confidence of a region that had been patient for a long time and was now watching its patience pay off. Capital was flowing in new directions. Cultural weight was redistributing. The map of influence was being redrawn, and Hispania was holding one of the pens.

Hector Ortega held several pens. He had allegiances across most of Hispania and into some areas of Persia — connections maintained through money, through ego, and through the particular kind of loyalty that men of his class purchase rather than earn. Information was of no matter to him other than another gold coin to pay for it. He did not deal in nuance. He dealt in outcomes. The world, as Hector understood it, was organized into people who had the resources to shape events and people who did not, and the distance between those two categories was the only geography that interested him.

He was, by most accounts, a man who had a great deal going for him. That he had been able to marry Alissia Klaros was evidence enough of this — Alissia was not a woman who came cheaply or easily, in any sense of those words, and the fact of their union spoke to a Hector who had once possessed something beyond mere money. Charm, perhaps. Vision. The specific magnetism of a man building something he believed in.

By the time of the carpenter, some of that had curdled into something simpler and more dangerous: an obsession with empire in the image of his ethno-cultural identity, and a sensitivity to opposition that had stopped being proportionate.

---

The carpenter did not have a name, according to most accounts. He was a commoner in the straightforward sense — a man who worked with his hands, who built things, who existed at a distance from the circles Hector moved in that should, under normal conditions, have been insurmountable. Commoners and royalty do not interact with each other, whatsoever. The geography of class is usually its own protection, operating in both directions.

The carpenter had been building houses. This was his work, his competence, his contribution to the physical world. He was good at it, presumably, in the way that men who have done a thing long enough and seriously enough become good at it without needing anyone to validate the fact.

The problem was that the carpenter had begun to think about who the houses were for.

The financial interests of Hispania and Persia had been moving into the American market with increasing visibility, and the carpenter, being a man who paid attention to the material conditions of his life, had noticed. From his perspective, the calculation was not complicated. His labor, applied to these projects, would produce one of two outcomes: vanity projects for people with more money than they needed, or housing for immigrants whose arrival was itself a product of geopolitical forces that had very little interest in the welfare of men like him. He held nothing personal against people from Hispania or Persia as people. The carpenter was a populist in the orthodox sense — his objection was structural, not ethnic. He did not want to inadvertently work for the interests of foreign competitors that gave very little care for people like him. He declined to continue.

This was, in practical terms, a carpenter declining work. It was a quiet act. It made no noise. It threatened nothing.

Hector Ortega was deeply offended.

---

This is the part of the story that requires some accounting, because the offense itself is revealing.

A man of Hector's position, receiving word that an unnamed carpenter in a disenfranchised American economy had declined to build houses for his financial interests, had available to him a range of responses. Indifference was the most logical. The carpenter was a commoner. The carpenter's labor was replaceable. The carpenter's opinion of Hispania and Persia was shared by a significant portion of the American population and had been for some time.

But Hector operated on his ego, and ego does not perform cost-benefit analyses. What Hector heard, in the carpenter's refusal, was not a practical decision made by a man protecting his own interests. What he heard was an insult to the image of empire he had spent his life constructing and defending. The carpenter, by simply declining, had implied that the empire was something a person could reasonably decline — that it was not inevitable, not universally desirable, not the natural order of things asserting itself. This was intolerable.

He sent for people. The initiative moved on multiple tracks simultaneously — there was evidence of movements to harm the carpenter's reputation, to damage his finances, to make the practical conditions of his life worse in ways that could not be easily traced back to a single origin. Whether physical harm was also on the table was a question that the historical record leaves open and that the people involved were careful not to answer directly.

The carpenter, being a commoner without institutional protection, was vulnerable to all of these tracks. That was the architecture of the operation. A man without connections, without capital, without the kind of name that generates consequences when it is damaged — such a man could be made to feel the weight of Hector Ortega's displeasure without Hector ever having to formally acknowledge that the weight was his.

---

What Hector did not calculate, or calculated incorrectly, was the chain reaction.

He already had enemies. Men of his reach and his methods accumulate enemies the way ships accumulate barnacles — gradually, invisibly, until the weight of them becomes a navigational problem. His enemies were, for the most part, within his class or close enough to it that the usual rules of engagement applied. There were understood limits. There were protocols. The game had a grammar.

The carpenter existed outside all of that. And in reaching outside his class to apply pressure to a man who had no formal means of fighting back, Hector had done something that men of empire periodically do and periodically regret: he had opened a door.

The doors of class, when they swing in one direction, tend to swing in both. The same opening that allowed Hector's influence to reach down into the world of commoners allowed other things to travel the other way — grievances, alliances, information moving through channels that Hector's money could not monitor because Hector's money had never needed to monitor them before. People who had watched the carpenter's situation and drawn their own conclusions. People who had been waiting for evidence of exactly this kind of overreach, for reasons that had nothing to do with the carpenter and everything to do with Hector.

He had made enemies outside his class. Those enemies, by default, could not reach him. He had, in effect, opened the doors for foreign threats to come to his home to roost — to his death.

The carpenter, for his part, likely never knew the full scope of what his refusal had set in motion. He had made a practical decision about his labor and his conscience, and somewhere above him, in the altitude where men like Hector Ortega conducted their affairs, that decision had become a stone dropped into still water, the rings expanding outward until they reached shores the carpenter had never seen and could not have named.

This is, in its way, the oldest story about empire there is.

The commoner declines.

The emperor takes it personally.

And the doors, once opened, do not close again on command.
